2. Related work
Multiple techniques have been developed to automatically extract 
network information from various types of remotely sensed data. Many 
machine learning techniques, including neural networks, can success­
fully extract useful information from satellite and aerial imagery as well 
as other data sources such as road vehicle-mounted sensors. The latter 
can include data in two or higher dimensions such as LiDAR, front-view 
camera imaging and GPS tracing.
Road-level graph extraction. Current network extraction research can 
be categorised into road-level extraction and extraction at higher level of 
detail. Initial road-level extraction studies focused on using pixel-wise 
segmentation as an intermediate stage, before applying post- 
processing algorithms to vectorize the network and to correct for to­
pology errors. Examples include Mattyus, Luo and Urtasun (2017), who 
use the Ramer–Douglas–Peucker algorithm to output a piecewise linear 
approximation of road topology using a road segmentation mask based 
on aerial imagery. Predicted topology was improved by identifying 
missing links between nearby leaf nodes and taking advantage of the A* 
shortest-path algorithm to correct for discontinuities. Subsequent 
studies focused on extracting network graphs directly, using point-based 
iterative graph growing procedures and Road Point Network (RP-Net) 
while maintaining alignment as by Tan et al. (2020), or by extracting 
complete networks non-recurrently as by He et al. (2020). Studies 
extracting network information at higher detail have focused on 
extracting attribute information of road segments. Examples include: 
Azimi et al. (2019) who use Wavelet-enhanced Symmetric Fully Con­
volutional Neural Networks (FCNNs) to efficiently and reliably extract 
lane markings; Xu, Sun and Liu (2021) who extract kerb lines with 
transformers and imitation learning and achieves promising results, 
especially in terms of maintaining connectivity; (He et al. (2020)) who 
combine Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Graph Neural 
Networks (GNNs) to utilise spatial correlation to overcome limitations of 
narrow receptive field and occlusion in inferring lane counts; and Najjar, 
Kaneko and Miyanaga (2017) who achieved an accuracy of 78 % in 
predicting road safety using CNNs trained on traffic incidents in New 
York.
Lane-level graph extraction using non-aerial sensory data. Multiple 
studies that extract lane-level topology use road vehicle sensory data, for 
example by using LiDAR (Geiger et al., 2014; Homayounfar et al., 2019), 
GPS-trajectories (Roeth, Zaum and Brenner, 2016) and camera imagery 
(Philion and Fidler, 2020; Jia et al., 2024). Some studies use a combi­
nation of input sensory data, such as LiDAR and camera imagery 
Karlsson et al. (2023). Schmidt et al. (2023) estimates centimetre- 
accurate high-definition maps using a road-level graph and high- 
definition map annotations with partial coverage using knowledge 
distillation, where a smaller, more efficient model (called the “student”) 
is trained to replicate the behaviour of a larger, more complex model 
(the “teacher”). Zürn, Posner and Burgard (2024) removes the need for 
hand-drawn annotations by estimating lane-level graphs using birds-eye 
view vehicle tracklets, while Chitta, Dauner and Geiger (2024) devel­
oped a generative model to synthesise simulation-ready lane networks.
Lane-level graph extraction using aerial imagery. A more recent 
research direction is the extraction of lane-level network topologies 
using only aerial imagery as input. This research track has been seen 
limited exploration so far. He and Balakrishnan (2022) developed 
LaneExtraction, a staged modelling pipeline using a lane segmentation 
model and vectorisation algorithm to identify lanes, regression to 
determine lane direction, and a binary classification model to determine 
connections between each lane at intersections. Buchner et al. (2023)
improved results compared to LaneExtraction by using segmentation 
and iterative graph growing networks to predict lane specific trajec­
tories based on vehicle starting points, which are merged to form global 
networks. Blayney et al. (2024) achieve smooth geometries and 
direction continuity by estimating lane networks using B´ezier curves. 
Yao et al. (2024) estimates lane-level vertices as an initial step, followed 
by a vertex connection classifier step based on nearest neighbour prin­
ciples. Ruiz et al. (2024) improves segmentation-based lane-detection at 
non-intersection areas by using a diffusion model. Lane segmentation is 
performed using D-LinkNet and LaneExtraction techniques and the 
refinement is conducted using a diffusion model which leads to 
improvement of geometry- and connectivity-based F1 scores in non- 
intersection areas, surpassing previous methods. Vijverberg, Beers and 
With (2024) estimates approach lanes and intersection conflict areas by 
applying a based segmentation approach using birds-eye imagery, as 
well as a combination of birds-eye view and vehicle-mounted camera 
imagery to combat the issue of occlusion. Furthermore, they combine 
aerial input with domain knowledge which incorporate prior knowledge 
about intersections, using segmentation masks and line extraction 
techniques, and merging using the Highest Confidence First algorithm to 
accurately derive drive lines between painted striping lines.
Contribution. In contrast to the recent research using aerial imagery, 
we additionally use a road-level graph extracted from OpenStreetMap 
(OSM) to derive locations of intersections and their arm bearings. 
Instead of relying on segmentation or direct graph extraction we use 
oriented object detection and multi-input classification models to pre­
dict lanes, their attributes, and the network topology by predicting the 
adjacency matrix for each intersection. Similar to Vijverberg, Beers and 
With (2024), the oriented object detection adds domain knowledge to 
the modelling pipeline. Another key contribution of this work is the 
focus on estimating topologies at signalized intersections, where topol­
ogy patterns can be particularly challenging.
3. Methods and Materials
3.1. Modelling pipeline
The overall deep neural network model pipeline is illustrated in 
Fig. 1. It consists of the lane object detection, lane detection, and turn 
detection models. These are applied sequentially, with the output 
probabilities from the lane object detection feeding into the lane 
detection model, and whose output probabilities feed into the turn 
detection model. The output from each sub-model is not binarized into 
discrete outcomes as this will maintain information contained in the 
predicted probability distribution.
The lane object detection model takes rotated imagery as input to 
predict lane objects. The lane objects include: arrow markings (left, 
right, right-left, right-through, right-through-left, through, through- 
left); bus lane marking; cars (forward, backward, other); cycle lane 
marking; large vehicle (forward, backward, other); horizontal line 
marking (stop line, other); vertical line markings (dashed lane separator, 
solid lane separator, median, parking delineator, other); wide medians 
(painted, physical), other road marking; pedestrian crossing (signalized, 
zebra); speed marking; split island; and painted verge. Rotation angles 
are given by the intersection arm bearings derived from the simplified 
OSM road-level graph. The prediction probabilities for each lane object 
class are summed along the image x-axis and used together with imagery 
and bearing differences to the other intersection arms to predict a set of 
lane attributes for 22 lane slots along the image x-axis. The width of each 
lane slot is set to correspond to a typical ground distance width of a 
traffic lane. Lane slot attribute predictions for each arm, together with 
intersection imagery and arm bearings, are used to predict the turns. 
Intersection arms and their bearings are exemplified in Fig. 2. A turn is 
defined as a connected lane slot-pair and is represented by the adjacency 
matrix. The aim of adjacency matrix format is to produce simultaneous 
turn predictions and utilise the correlation in intersection network to­
pology. As a final stage, inconsistent turns are filtered using a heuristic 
to remove inconsistent predictions. All three models use ResNet (He 
et al., 2016) architectures for their computer vision CNN backbones. The 
lane and turn detection models make additional use of MLPs and

3.2. Dataset
Road-level graph and aerial imagery In this study, we leverage open 
and publicly accessible datasets, which is a significant strength, as it 
ensures the scalability, reproducibility, and broad applicability of our 
approach. Two datasets are required for the study, OSM data for the 
road-level graph and aerial imagery as inputs to the neural network 
models. OSM data was extracted using the OSMNX Python package 
(Boeing, 2017), while the aerial imagery was extracted using the pub­
licly available aerial imagery Web Map Tile Service (WMTS) covering 
metropolitan areas of New South Wales. 894 signalized intersections 
were identified based on OSM tag data and aerial imagery was extracted 
to capture the intersections and each of their arms separately. The arm 
imagery was rotated using arm bearings from the simplified OSM graph. 
Rotation of the images enables annotation of oriented objects, such as 
annotating a car approaching an intersection separately to a car exiting 
an intersection along the same arm.

3.3. Lane object detection
The lane object detection model takes the rotated intersection arm 
images as input and is trained against the lane object bounding box 
annotations in the COCO annotation format (a standard object detection 
annotation format). The Faster R-CNN ResNet object detector with pre- 
trained weights was used with the Detectron2 framework developed by 
Meta. The output is the bounding box predictions, including pixel 
centroid coordinates, bounding box widths and heights, and class labels 
with prediction probabilities. The Detectron2 default Focal Loss function 
(L F) (Lin et al., 2017) is used for the image feature extractor in the lane 
object detection model.
3.4. Lane detection
The lane detection model predicts lane slot attributes for each arm 
lane slot, taking arm imagery, summed lane object detection probabil­
ities, and arm bearing offsets as input. The object detection probabilities 
are summed for each object class and projected onto the image x-axis. To 
reduce data size, these projections are spatially compressed from the 
image pixels to a vector of length 220, corresponding to 10 per lane slot. 
Fig. 3 provides an illustrated example of summed and compressed lane 
object probabilities. Bearing offsets are calculated as the angular dif­
ference between the bearing of one intersection arm and the bearings of 
the other arms, measured in a clockwise direction. The lane slot attri­
butes include a binary lane, turn, and slip lane flags, as well as multi- 
class lane type flags, with approach lane, exit lane, and no lane as 
classes. The binary lane flag is provided in addition to the lane type class 
flags to improve the model performance. Data was augmented to arti­
ficially increase the sample size by randomly shifting input and label 
data along the horizontal axis. This also aims to mitigate the effect of the 
neural network learning labels based on lane slot location.
The lane detection model architecture is summarised in Fig. 4. It 
combines blocks of convolutional and dense layers, and a ResNet block. 
The ResNet block takes the arm image input, while a combined con­
volutional and dense block takes the concatenated and flatten summed 
lane object probabilities and bearing offsets as input. The outputs from 
these two blocks are concatenated and fed into an additional block with 
dense layers, whose output is reshaped into the lane slot attribute 
matrix.
Following the approach by He and Balakrishnan (2022), we employ a 
combined cross-entropy and soft dice loss, which has been shown to be 
effective for classification tasks involving imbalanced classes (Galdran, 
Carneiro, and Ballester, 2022). Each loss type is computed separately for 
each of the attribute categories and are subsequently aggregated into a 
single loss value. The loss weight values were optimized during the 
model tuning process.
The lane data is highly imbalanced. For example, the probability of a 
lane slot containing a lane is ~ 17 %, meaning that the negative class 
(also called the background class) is about 4.8 times more prevalent than 
the positive class. To avoid biased training and counteract the imbalance 
problem, weights based on the negative-to-positive ratios were incor­
porated into the cross-entropy (CE) loss function terms.
3.5. Turn detection
The turn detection model produces the probabilities of connections 
between every lane slot combination by predicting the adjacency ma­
trix. It takes as input an image centred on the intersection, the lane slot 
attribute matrix predictions for each of the intersection arms and 
bearing values for each intersection arm.
The turn detection model architecture is summarised in Fig. 5. It 
combines blocks of Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLPs) and the ResNet CNN 
block. The ResNet block processes the intersection image, while an MLP 
block takes the stacked lane slot attribute probabilities and arm bearings

Like the lane detection model, the turn detection model employs a 
combined cross entropy (CE) and soft dice loss. It also uses a weighted 
cross-entropy loss terms to counteract the imbalance problem, which is 
even more pronounced for the turn detection model. Large areas of the 
adjacency matrix have very high N/P ratios. These correspond to the 
yellow areas in Fig. 6 where almost no positive cases exist. Training the 
model with weights for these areas proved difficult as the focus become 
on learning to predict these near-impossible cases. Therefore, the loss 
function was further adjusted to focus more on the area of interest, 
corresponding to the dark island areas in Fig. 5. The remaining area, 
defined as matrix cells with a N/P ratio value above a threshold, is 
classified as the ‘almost impossible’ area and was assigned a different 
weighting for the CE loss. The specific weighting and N/P ratio threshold 
were determined during model tuning.

3.7. Implementation details
The lane object detection model was trained for 6000 iterations with 
a batch size of 5 images, using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) 
(Ruder, 2016) as optimiser and an initial learning rate of 0.001. Frozen 
pre-trained ResNet parameters were used, except for the final dense 
layer which was trained. For the lane detection and turn detection 
models, Adam (Kingma and Ba, 2014) was used as optimiser and early 
stopping was used to avoid overfitting. Additional hyperparameter 
values were determined using the Optuna hyperparameter tuning 
framework (Akiba et al., 2019) based on the Tree-structured Parzen 
Estimator algorithm (Bergstra, Yamins and Cox, 2013). These include 
values for batch size, learning rate, loss function term weights, cap 
values for the class imbalance weights, and the threshold used to 
determine the ‘almost impossible’ area of the adjacency matrix.

4. Results
In this section, we evaluate the performance of each sub-model using 
precision, recall, and F1 as key metrics.
4.1. Lane object detection
For object detection tasks both the location of the bounding boxes 
and the class accuracy is evaluated. The accuracy of the size and location 
of the bounding boxes is measured using the Intersection-over-Union 
(IoU) metric between prediction and labels, while class accuracy is re­
ported using precision. A combined Average Precision (AP) is evaluated 
as the average precision across a range of IoU thresholds. The precision 
across five IoU thresholds along with the corresponding AP measure for 
key lane object classes is provided in Table 1. It suggests satisfactory 
precision for most classes for IoU thresholds at or below 0.7. The Stop 
line class is an exception and has comparatively lower performance.
4.2. Lane detection
The performance metrics for the lane detection model are based on 
the lane slot attribute predictions. A binarisation threshold yielding the 
same number of positive predictions and labels is selected for evalua­
tion. The resulting metric values being summarised in Table 2. The 
model effectively predicts the presence of lanes but struggles with dis­
tinguishing between approach and exit lanes. While it can make initial 
turn predictions with reasonable accuracy, it performs better at pre­
dicting through movements compared to left and right turns.
4.3. Turn detection
The turn model outputs a lane-to-lane adjacency matrix, where each 
cell indicates whether a turn from an approach slot to an exit slot is 
allowed. At this stricter target, we obtain an overall F1 of 0.60 (left 0.48, 
through 0.66, right 0.57). In addition to reporting these adjacency 
scores, we also assess performance by deriving lane types per arm. This 
evaluation method allows for a more realistic (and less pessimistic) way 
of evaluating performance. Consider the case where an exact lane slot 
connection was not correctly predicted, but a positive prediction was 
made with one of the neighbouring lane slots either the approach or exit 
arm. Practically, this prediction could still be considered a good pre­
diction but would constitute a false positive during metric calculations. 
To mitigate this issue and avoid overly pessimistic performance assess­
ments, the evaluation focuses on the number of correctly predicted lane 
types rather than strict lane slot connections.
The lane types are extracted from the adjacency matrix as follows: 1) 
For each approach lane slot (represented by the adjacency matrix rows) 
left, through, and right turns are extracted based on the position of the 
column, where the 22 first columns represent left turns, the following 22 
through turns, and the last 22 right turns; 2) Lane types are subsequently 
defined as the type of turns allowed for each approach lane slot. For 
example, if an approach lane slot contains both a left turn and a through 
turn the lane type is a left-through turn, here denoted as an LT-type turn, 
and an approach lane slot with only a right turn as an R-type lane, and so 
on. This classification results in 7 approach lane types. 3) The number of 
lanes by approach lane type is compared against ground truth and forms 
the basis of the metric calculations, meaning that the location and lane 
slot of the lane types are ignored. Metric values for lane types, as well as 
for number of turns and overall number of approach lanes using an 
optimised binarisation threshold are summarised in Table 3. The results 
show that number of the model can predict number of approach lanes 
and turns better than the number of lane types.
The performance of the unprocessed predictions (hereafter referred 
to as ‘raw’) is compared with the processed turns where the inconsistent 
predictions are removed, and with a simple rule-based model serving as 
benchmark to help contextualise the results. The rule-based model 
operates under the following rules: Each arm of a four-way intersection 
contains one LT-type and one TR-type lane; a three-way intersection 
contains one arm with one T-type lane and one TR-type lane, one arm 
with one T-type lane and one TL-type lane, and one arm with one L-type 
lane and one R-type lane.
F1 metrics comparing the number of predicted approach lanes, 
number of turns, and number of lane types are represented in Table 4. 
The default rule-based model generally shows better performance, 
particularly in predicting overall lane counts and Left turning lanes, 
although the neural network performs better for T-type and TR-type 
lanes. Notably, the default model does not predict LTR-type or LR-type 
lanes—rare lane types that the neural network also struggles to pre­
dict. Additionally, performance tends to be lower for the less common L- 
type and R-type turns (i.e., dedicated left and right turn lanes). All 
models can better predict the overall number of lanes and turns better 
than specific lane types, which is expected. The similarity between the 
raw and processed results suggests that the raw predictions may not 
contain a significant number of inconsistent turns that would be filtered 
out by the heuristic.

5. Discussion
Despite moderate performance in predicting the adjacency matrix, 
the modelling pipeline recovers key topology aspects, including the 
number of numbers of lanes and allowed turns for each arm. It is less 
able to predict detailed lane layouts, including rarer lane types such as 
dedicated left and right turn lanes. The post-processing heuristic had 
minimal impact on results, likely indicating the raw results are mostly 
consistent.
Overall, lane-type recovery is promising but needs improvement 
before practical use. While it does not outperform a simple rule-based 
model on this dataset, the pipeline is scalable, adaptable, and exten­
sible to unseen and non-standard layouts. Technical advantages include 
predicting topology directly without geometry extraction, leveraging 
lane-object cues, and learning correlations between turns.
Class imbalance limits both learning and evaluation. Results suggest 
the model improves on less common layouts when there is enough 
sample size: T and TR lanes perform better than in the default model, 
while L and R show gains but remain below common types and the 
default model. For the latter cases, learning is likely constrained as a of 
limited sample size. Very rare cases such as LTR and LR, about 2 percent 
each, are not learned at all. This points to a need for a more varied and 
balanced dataset to cover a wider set of layouts. The imbalance may also 
inflate the default model performance. For example, LT is the most 
common lane type and records the strongest default-model results, 
indicating the baseline is skewed toward common layouts and less 
reliable for rarer types.

rarer layouts and can inflate baseline performance on common cases. It 
is restricted to signalized intersections in a single region, which limits

Additional limitations include vulnerability to occlusion and to non- 
standard intersection layouts such as one-way configurations, offset 
geometries, and unusually shaped intersections. As illustrated in Fig. 8, 
these conditions often obscure critical visual cues like lane markings, 
stop lines, or vehicle trajectories. Such conditions are not rare anomalies 
but frequently encountered in real-world urban networks. While the use 
of rotation-normalised imagery and multi-class lane object detection 
mitigates some effects, aerial imagery alone cannot fully resolve occlu­
sion or severe perspective distortion.
A related concern is the absence of an explicit ablation or 
component-level analysis. Although not formally conducted, insights 
can be drawn from comparative evidence in Table 4. The minimal dif­
ference between raw and processed results suggests that the PPH 
maintains correctness while eliminating logically inconsistent turns, 
thus enhancing topological validity rather than boosting average F1 
scores. Qualitative analysis (Fig. 6) further indicates that most residual 
errors originate from the lane detection stage, which directly influences 
the subsequent turn prediction accuracy. This implies that improve­
ments in lane detection, particularly distinguishing approach versus exit 
lanes, would likely yield the largest overall performance gains. Hence, 
while the current study focuses on end-to-end evaluation, a detailed

The geographic focus on intersections in New South Wales, partic­
ularly Sydney, offers consistency in imagery and annotation but natu­
rally constrains generalization claims. The methodology, however, is not 
city-specific: all inputs (OSM-derived road graphs and publicly available 
aerial imagery) are globally available, and model parameters are 
agnostic to regional conventions. We expect the approach to generalize 
well to other urban areas with similar aerial imagery resolution and 
traffic marking standards, although performance may vary with 
differing lane marking styles, lighting conditions, or intersection ge­
ometries. A meaningful next step is to apply transfer learning or few-shot 
fine-tuning to small samples from other regions, such as other Australian 
or European cities, to systematically evaluate robustness under varying 
environmental and cultural driving contexts. This extension is 
straightforward within the current pipeline and will be pursued in future 
work.
We will also explore replacing or complementing the OSM-derived 
road graph with a predicted road-level graph, adding road-surface or 
lane segmentation maps as inputs, and testing alternative targets to the 
adjacency matrix, such as a binary lane classifier. Finally, extending the 
method to predict lane geometries and to extract networks outside in­
tersections would enable construction of global lane-level graphs.
7. Conclusion
This work developed and compared deep learning methodologies for 
extracting lane-level network topology at signalized intersections. A 
staged model pipeline is developed consisting of a lane object detection 
model, a lane detection model, and a turn detection model. Output 
probabilities are relayed down-stream between each sub-model, with 
the final model outputting the adjacency matrix containing probabilities 
for each lane-to-lane connection. We adjust the loss functions counteract 
effects from data imbalance. The models are trained and evaluated
